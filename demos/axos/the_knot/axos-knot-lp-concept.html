Why a narrative-flow calculator, not a static dashboard
  • The card is structured as a story top-to-bottom: ① you're making a smart move → ② here's what you'll deposit → ③ here's what you add together each month → ④ here's how long you'll let it grow → ⑤ here's what it becomes → ⑥ here are the dreams it funds. Each row has one job, and the punchline (the combined balance) lands at the visual climax.
  • Three-slider model. Slider 1: lump-sum deposit ($1K-$150K). Slider 2: monthly joint contribution ($0-$2,000/mo, defaults to $0). Slider 3: time horizon (1-10 years). All three feed one big reveal — the combined balance. Cause-and-effect is immediately legible: drag anything right, the reveal grows.
  • Monthly contribution defaults to $0 on purpose. The base case is honest: just compound the deposit. But the moment someone drags that slider — even to $200/month — the 10-year balance jumps dramatically. That's the "we keep adding to it together" moment. The chart of compounding power, with one finger.
  • The 10-year time slider is intentional. Five years undersells compounding ($35K → $43K with no contributions). Combine 10 years + $300/month contributions and the same starting balance reaches $96K+. The slider is showing them the value of staying and committing — that's a feature, not a problem. Default lands at year 5.
  • Visual concept: two interlocking gold rings. Wedding bands are both gold. Universal symbol for "joined" with zero ambiguity, perfectly on-brand for the Knot partnership. Both "You" and "Partner" labels in matching gold — equal partners.
  • Dream chips are inspiration, not interaction. Below the reveal: Honeymoon · Down payment · First baby · A real vacation · Emergency cushion · The next chapter. These are the answer to "what does this balance become?" — the emotional payoff after the math.
Stolen / inspired by
  • Wealthfront: right-side product preview as hero (vs. left-side text only). Their highest-converting layout.
  • Ally Bank: embedded above-fold rate calculator with slider UI.
  • Vanguard / Fidelity retirement calculators: the deposit + monthly + time slider trio. Familiar to anyone who's ever projected a retirement balance.
  • Nerdwallet's compound interest tool: the single-number reveal with "you earned X" subhead pattern.
  • SoFi: the "smart move" badge / confidence-building micro-positioning above the input.
Why the math is honest and the framing is safe
  • Formula: monthly compounding loop — for each month over the period, balance = balance × (1 + APY/12) + monthly contribution. This matches Axos's actual compounding cadence on Savings products. Conservative on the contribution timing (end-of-month, not beginning-of-month). The number is defensible to any compliance reviewer.
  • The hero number is the combined balance, not interest earned. Balance growth is what customers actually care about. "$43,015" beats "you'll earn $8,015 in interest" because it shows what their money becomes, not what's added to it. Interest earned is in the subhead — present but not the headline.
  • The "earned" number is interest only, net of contributions. If they add $300/mo for 5 years, that $18,000 of contributions isn't counted as "earned" — only the actual interest the APY produces. Honest math, no inflated promise.
  • "Save together, earn more together" is the headline copy — it implies a doubling effect without making a literal 2x claim. Compliance-clean. The math below proves it: $500K joint FDIC coverage (literally doubled), two paychecks both qualifying at 4.21%, combined balance compounding from a higher base, and now both partners contributing monthly.
  • "More than at the average bank" pill below the reveal is the comparative kicker. Uses generic "average bank" framing (not named competitors) to avoid FCBA-style claim challenges.
Where this beats the rest of the category
  • Chase, Wells Fargo, BofA: no joint-aware product framing on any LP. Joint accounts are buried in account-type FAQs.
  • Ally: rate-led but not couple-aware. Our angle is structurally unavailable to them without a campaign reskin.
  • Capital One 360: 4.20% on savings only, no checking. Our integrated product means deposits earn from day one.
  • Marcus by Goldman: single-slider calculator. We add both time and recurring-contribution dimensions, which is what makes compounding visceral and "saving together" feel real.

Why this section is a static comparison, not a second calculator
  • The hero calculator already does the interactive work. A second calculator here would be redundant — users don't drag two sliders to do the same thing twice.

  • Anchoring at $50,000 (rather than $25K) is intentional — it's a believable post-wedding combined balance and produces a more dramatic earnings gap. Smaller anchors blunt the contrast.
Compliance considerations on named competitors
  • All four named-competitor rates are standard published rates as of Q2 2026 (not promotional, not relationship-based). The footnote calls this out explicitly to keep the comparison defensible.
  • FCBA / Reg DD compliance review needed before live to confirm methodology and to lock the as-of date for the rates shown.
  • If compliance is uncomfortable with named competitors, fallback: "Big Bank average," "Online bank average," with a footnote citing the source. Less vivid but defensible.
Visual hierarchy choices
  • Axos row gets visual lift: warm-gold gradient background, accent-orange top border, slightly larger earnings number. Reads as "and here's the winner" without needing to say it.

  • Capital One row sits between the big banks and Axos to show that even the "good" online savings rate (3.5%) is still meaningfully below Axos.
