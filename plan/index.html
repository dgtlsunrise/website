A Plan for
Our Family

On why the last three years changed how I think about risk, why I believe AI is about to reshape the way most people work, and what I'm doing about it — for all of us.

By Noel Churchill  ·  May 2026

I want to explain what I'm doing, because the people who matter most deserve a real explanation — not a summary, not reassurances, but the reasoning itself. You have watched our family navigate a difficult few years. You have been generous and supportive in ways I don't take lightly. So I want to be completely honest with you about where things stand, what I'm planning, and why I believe this is the right move for all of us.

This is going to be a long read. I'm asking you to stay with it, because the details matter and because I want to answer your questions before you have to ask them.

We previously all sat down together — Daria, me, Kris and Tom — and talked about the need to have a real plan. This is that plan. It has taken me a long time to think it all the way though and put it together, and I want you to understand every part of it.

What I built,
and why it was
so hard to let go

For most of its thirteen years, RainbowOPTX was genuinely good to me. At its peak, it provided a fantastic lifestyle — financial prosperity, creative autonomy, and the particular satisfaction of having built something from nothing, on my own terms and my own capital. I grew it into a global operation, scaled it through every challenge that comes with running a consumer brand, and did it entirely without outside investors or a safety net beneath me. I'm proud of what it was at its best.

But it offered something else too, something I needed more than ever: flexibility. When Leif was born, I became his primary parent at around two and a half months old. There was no other kind of work that would have allowed me to be present the way I needed to be. Self-employment, whatever its financial inconsistencies, gave me that. I could structure my days around him. That mattered enormously, and it was a real part of why I held on even as the business became harder to sustain. It felt like the only option I had at the time.

The last three years were difficult in ways I didn't anticipate. Circumstances changed, the business suffered, and by the time I sold it in late 2025, what had once been a source of pride and stability had become a prolonged grind. The financial unpredictability of those years took a real toll — not just on my bank account, but psychologically. I know you've seen that. And I want you to know how much it has meant that you've been there for us.

Since the sale, I've been consulting for Axos Bank, a Top 100 U.S. financial institution. The work is meaningful and the people are good. But it is still consulting — still variable, still without a floor, still the particular anxiety of not knowing how long the contract will last.

I want to be honest about the urgency here. The Axos consulting has not been enough to cover our expenses. I have been trying to build more consulting clients, and it has been harder than I hoped. The financial pressure is real, and it is part of why I am moving on this with the seriousness that I am. This is not a plan I am pursuing in case things get hard. Things are already difficult, and this is the plan.

Leif is two and a half years old now. He can go to early childhood education. The practical reason I held onto self-employment — the flexibility it gave me to care for him — no longer applies in the same way. And the level of risk that maybe made sense under different circumstances doesn't make sense anymore. The question I've been working on is: what comes next for us, and how do I do it right?

The fire at
Le Constellation

Before I get to the plan, I want to explain what changed my thinking — because this isn't just about the last three years. It's about what I see coming.

On New Year's Eve 2025, a bar called Le Constellation in Crans-Montana — a ski resort in the Swiss Alps — caught fire during the party. A waitress, sitting on another person's shoulders, held a champagne bottle topped with a lit sparkler near a wooden ceiling. The ceiling caught. The fire spread. Then something happened that fire scientists call a "flashover" — a threshold beyond which everything in a room ignites almost simultaneously.

Forty people were killed. Over a hundred were injured.

What struck me when I first saw the footage wasn't the tragedy itself — it was the moments before the flashover. People standing there, drinks in hand, watching flames spread across the ceiling with something like amusement. Not panic. Not urgency. Just curiosity. Maybe the assumption that someone else would handle it. Maybe the domesticated modern human tendency to underestimate danger until it's too late to do anything about it.

Le Constellation bar, Crans-Montana, Switzerland. New Year's Eve 2025.
40 killed, 115 injured. From sparkler to flashover in minutes.

That video is an analogy for the way I think about this moment in time as artificial intelligence engulfs our world.

"AI is going to be constructive in so many ways. But it is still going to be the fire that burns down all the previous ways of doing things — and most people are watching with amusement instead of heading for the exit."

I work with AI every day. I build with it, experiment with it running models locally on my own hardware, and have watched it improve rapidly. I know exactly what it can do. It can take over your computer — literally see your full screen, click around, navigate software, and complete tasks a human would do — with full AI intelligence, at a fraction of the cost, and without human needs. This is not theoretical. It is happening now.

In March 2026, Anthropic — the company that makes the AI assistant I use most, and one of the leading AI labs in the world — published a study mapping which jobs AI is already performing and which it is theoretically capable of performing. The findings were serious. They found that AI can theoretically cover most tasks in business, finance, management, computer science, math, legal work, and office administration. The researchers named the risk plainly: a potential "Great Recession for white-collar workers." Anthropic's own CEO has said the technology could displace half of entry-level white-collar work.

Most people are watching AI with something like amusement. It's interesting. It's impressive. They know it's a little scary. But the urgency hasn't registered yet. The sparklers are near the ceiling and everyone still has a drink in their hand.

I think we need to move toward the metaphorical exit — deliberately, while there was still time to choose where we land.

What I can
and can't know

Before I walk you through how I made this decision, I want to say something about how sure I actually am — because it would be easy to read what follows as more certain than it is.

I don't know the future. None of us do. I don't know for sure that this is the best plan. I think it is a genuinely good plan — I've done real research and thought it through carefully — but it comes with major tradeoffs and real disruption, and I can't promise you it's the best of all the paths we could take. I'd be lying if I said otherwise. I also can't promise you I'll be able to pull it off. The process is uncertain, and there are no guarantees.

But here's the thing I keep coming back to. Not knowing isn't a reason to do nothing. It is the exact reason a decision has to be made at all. If the future were certain, this wouldn't take any judgment — I'd just read the answer off the page. It's because I can't see clearly that I have to look as hard as I can, weigh what I see, and commit to a direction.

And when I look forward as honestly as I can, the specific thing I see is this: I believe AI is going to replace almost all computer-based work. Not someday — within the time horizon that matters for our family. The work I have built my expertise in, and the work most knowledge workers do, is going to be done by AI that can do it better, faster, and cheaper. I don't know exactly how fast. I don't know exactly how complete. But I am confident enough in the direction that I am not willing to stake our family's future on the hope that I'm wrong.

"I'm not doing this because I'm certain it's right. I'm doing it because I'm fairly certain that standing still is worse — and a worry that doesn't turn into action is just anxiety."

So I want to be clear about what this plan is. It is not me announcing that I've found the answer. It is my best attempt to respond to a future I can't predict but am genuinely concerned about. I am more sure that we need to take action than I am that this specific plan is perfect. And this is part of why I'm hoping for your involvement — not just your understanding. I would genuinely value your thinking on the pieces I might be getting wrong.

Three filters,
applied honestly

I have three primary criteria, and I applied them in sequence — not starting with an answer and working backward, but following the logic wherever it led.

Filter One

Not replaceable by AI

The first instinct most people will have — and I had it too — is to think of manual labor as the safe harbor. AI can't replace a plumber or a carpenter. That's true. But it buys five to ten years at most before robotics and physical automation catches up, and it is not a long-term answer.

More importantly, it is going to be everyone else's backup plan too. That is the problem. A backup plan only works if you're one of the few people doing it. When a large share of knowledge-worker jobs disappear and millions of displaced professionals all follow the same path of least resistance into service sector work, the competition for those positions becomes ferocious. Wages get pushed down. Hours get cut. People who have been in the trades for decades get priority. A marketing professional in their forties who has never worked in a trade is not going to outcompete someone who has done nothing else.

What I was looking for was work that is difficult to automate not because it's physical, but because it's relational. Work that requires human-to-human trust built over time, complex negotiation, professional judgment developed through years of practice, and the kind of institutional credibility that a language model cannot replicate. AI can assist with almost everything. There are very few categories where it cannot replace the person doing the work — and I wanted to be in one of them.

Remaining after Filter One: relationship-based professional work, government sector, skilled trades (short-term only)

Filter Two

Protected from volatility

The second filter isn't just recession-resistance — it's volatility-resistance, which is a broader and more honest concept. I'm not only worried about a traditional economic downturn. I'm worried about a more complicated kind of turbulence: AI displacing large numbers of workers, who then have less money to spend, which affects the businesses that served them, which ripples outward to every professional who depended on those businesses as clients. And layered on top of that: genuine global instability — rising geopolitical tensions, unpredictable policy shifts, and the general sense that the world is in a period of significant transition.

Private sector work — even good private sector work — is exposed to all of this. Businesses lose customers when customers lose jobs. Consulting engagements get cut when budgets tighten. Even stable companies are not immune to the knock-on effects of broad economic disruption. We have lived through three years of exactly this kind of income volatility, and I am not interested in more of it.

The organizations that are genuinely insulated — structurally, not just temporarily — are those funded by the federal government, particularly for services that don't stop when times are hard. Defense requirements do not pause during recessions. Security does not become optional during economic downturns. Government employment, especially in essential functions, offers a kind of stability that has no private sector equivalent.

Remaining after Filter Two: government employment and government-adjacent work

Filter Three

Real long-term upside

A stable government job with a predictable salary and full benefits would be a genuine improvement over the last three years. I want to be clear: it is a good answer, and if that is where this ends, our family will be better for it. I would be grateful for it, and I would serve well.

But I want to thrive. I want our family to thrive. Not at the expense of stability — stability comes first, always — but I strongly believe I can build something more than just comfortable, and I intend to try. So the third filter was: is there a path from here to something bigger? A second chapter that builds over time into something meaningful?

The answer I kept arriving at was defense technology. Global tensions are rising. U.S. military budgets are expanding significantly. A new generation of defense-tech companies — building drones, autonomous systems, and AI-powered national security tools — are growing at extraordinary speed, attracting enormous investment, and heading toward public offerings. The people who understand how the government buys things, from the inside, are rare. The demand for them is growing. That scarcity is the opportunity.

Remaining after Filter Three: federal acquisition within the defense sector

Where all three filters converge

Federal Acquisition
in the Defense Sector

Federal acquisition — the government function responsible for contracting everything the military buys, from drones to software to infrastructure — is exactly the kind of relationship-based, judgment-intensive, human-centered work that AI cannot replace. It is funded by the federal government, which means it is structurally insulated from economic volatility. And it is the precise credential that private defense-tech companies pay a significant premium to hire — because the people who know how the government makes buying decisions are few, and the demand for them is growing fast.

The obvious
question

The moment you read "federal contracting" and "Department of Defense," the reasonable question is: can someone with no prior government experience actually get one of these jobs? It's a fair question, and I want to answer it directly.

The short answer is yes — and here is why.

The Department of Defense has been facing a serious shortage of contracting professionals for years. In response, they created a hiring mechanism called Direct Hire Authority, which allows them to bypass the traditional competitive federal hiring process and bring in people directly from the public — including people with no prior government experience. These positions are genuinely open to people like me. I am applying for exactly the kind of position this authority was designed to fill.

My business background translates to federal contracting more directly than it might appear. For thirteen years I was the sole contracting authority for my brand. I negotiated supplier agreements, resolved disputes through formal written modifications, managed import compliance across multiple regulatory frameworks, conducted vendor market research and comparative analysis, structured a complex performance-based exit agreement, and coordinated multi-party payoff and lien releases at closing — all without outside lawyers or brokers doing it for me. The federal contracting framework has specific names for all of these activities, and my experience maps to them closely enough that the government's own reviewers are taking my application seriously.

I also meet the educational requirement. Federal contracting positions require a bachelor's degree with at least 24 semester hours of qualifying business coursework. My B.A. in Business Economics from UC Santa Barbara included 35.3 qualifying semester hours — exceeding the requirement.

I have already applied to multiple positions. One application has advanced to the hiring manager review stage. That is not a job offer — the process takes time and nothing is guaranteed — but it is a real signal that my background is being taken seriously at a GS-13 level, which is a senior position in the federal pay scale.

What federal employment actually looks like

A GS-13 Contract Specialist at a major DoD installation earns between $109,000 and $142,000 per year in base salary, before locality pay adjustments that add significantly depending on location. Federal employment includes comprehensive health insurance, a pension, retirement matching, generous leave, and job security that private sector work simply does not offer.

We would have a salary that arrives on schedule, benefits that don't depend on a good month, and the kind of predictability that the last three years have not had. That matters enormously — to me, and I think to you too.

The plan:
two moves

I engineered this backward — starting with my end goal and working out the clearest path to get there. It is two distinct moves, and each one sets up the next.

1

Now → 2029+

Enter federal acquisition at the DoD

Get a Contract Specialist role at the Department of Defense — the government side of defense procurement. Build the credentials that private defense-tech companies value most: federal contracting authority, experience with the specialized acquisition vehicles the government uses to buy cutting-edge technology quickly, a security clearance, and the professional network that comes from being inside the system.

I have already applied to multiple GS-1102 Contract Specialist positions across several major DoD commands. The federal government posts these openings continuously, and the Direct Hire Authority means my private-sector background is genuinely competitive.

2

2029+ → Onward

Exit to the private defense-tech sector

After building government-side credentials — contracting authority, clearance, acquisition expertise, relationships — use them to move to the private side. Companies like Anduril, Shield AI, and Palantir are actively recruiting people who understand how the government actually makes buying decisions, because those people are genuinely rare.

The target would be a senior role in government operations or business capture — the function that helps defense companies win government contracts. Compensation at that level typically runs between $200,000 and $250,000 in base salary, with meaningful equity in companies that are growing fast and heading toward public offerings. If Daria completes her CRNA path, our combined household income could change our family's trajectory entirely. That is the vision. The federal job is the foundation that makes it possible.

There is also a parallel track worth mentioning. I have applied for a Homeland Defender position with U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services — a federal role that is currently offering a $50,000 signing and retention bonus. This role would serve two purposes: it would address the immediate income situation by getting me into the federal employment system with a meaningful compensation package, and it would open up additional 1102 contracting positions that are only available to current federal employees. If this comes through before a direct contracting offer, I would take it — not as a substitute for the plan, but as a bridge that accelerates it and solves the near-term financial problem at the same time.

I want to be honest about what this plan is and what it isn't. Step 1 is achievable and I am already pursuing it seriously. Step 2 is the upside — the second chapter I believe is genuinely within reach if I execute Step 1 well. But if Step 2 never materializes, and I build a career in federal service, that is also a good outcome. A stable, well-compensated federal career is the foundation I am building toward first.

Federal employment comes with meaningful vacation time we could use to come visit, and wherever we land, you would be welcome as often as anyone wants to come. The distance would be real, but it would not be a wall.

I also want to be honest about the alternative, because staying in Coeur d'Alene is a real option and it deserves a fair accounting. If we stayed, I could pursue federal work locally — there are government positions in the Spokane area. Realistically, an entry-level federal role in our area would likely pay somewhere in the range of $60,000 to $77,000 to start, and with time and promotions could eventually reach six figures — though that would take several years, and the ceiling is meaningfully lower without access to the senior defense contracting roles that major installations support. On that income, combined with the stability of federal benefits, we could make it work. Especially once Daria finishes nursing school and starts earning. That is a real path.

But "making it work" is not the same as thriving. And the specific expertise I would build at a major defense installation — the clearance, the contracting authority, the OTA knowledge, the network — is not available locally. The second chapter, the one with real financial upside, requires being where the work is. I'm not presenting this as an easy call. I'm presenting it as the one that gives our family the best realistic chance at something more than comfortable.

Daria's nursing school

Daria is enrolled in a fully online, asynchronous program at Joyce University. Her coursework is not tied to any physical location — she can complete it from anywhere in the country, on her own schedule. A move does not interrupt her program at all.

Clinical rotations are the more nuanced piece. Joyce University arranges clinicals locally where possible, but they do not guarantee local placement — in fact, the most likely placement would require her to travel to Boise anyway. Clinical logistics are something every nursing student navigates wherever they live.

What changes meaningfully is the quality of hospitals available. The cities where these federal positions are concentrated are major metropolitan areas with large, high-volume hospital systems. Working at a Level 1 trauma center in a bigger city offers a complexity and volume of cases that accelerates the clinical experience top CRNA programs require. A federal salary, stable and predictable, also means Daria can focus on school without the financial anxiety that has shadowed the last three years. That is not a small thing.

CRNA is one of the highest-compensated specialties in all of healthcare — salaries that regularly exceed $200,000. If Daria and I both reach our respective goals, our household will be in an entirely different financial position than anything we have experienced. That is a realistic outcome of a plan that is already in motion.
